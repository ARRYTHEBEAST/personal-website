    Babur: Samarkand to Ayodhya

    Dec 9, 2025 · ~10 min read

    Having grown up in an Indian education system I was taught a lot about India’s history. One of the eras that we focused on was the Mughal Empire, and studying it made me come across the founder of that empire: Babur. All the history that we learned about Babur started from the Battle of Panipat between Babur and Ibrahim Lodi, when Babur enters India and usurps the throne at Delhi, setting up his Mughal Empire which goes on to dominate India until its eventual defeat against the Marathas. Babur’s origins are not deeply touched upon; it was just stated that he had Turkic and Mongolic ancestry and that he was from Samarkand in Uzbekistan. In this paper we will look at the details about Babur’s journey through the Silk Road, his travels from Samarkand to Delhi, and how that shaped his extremist ideology.

    Babur was born in the Ferghana Valley, a part of Transoxiana north of the Oxus River, a region rich in Turko-Persian culture and the heartland of Timurid heritage. He was a direct descendant of Timur and Genghis Khan. Samarkand, the ancient Timurid capital, loomed large in Babur’s imagination. In his memoir Baburnama he writes in awe: “Few cities in the civilized world are as pleasant as Samarkand.” He marvelled at its architecture and gardens, noting that “The city of Samarkand is an amazingly ornamented city,” with masjids and madrasas built by Timur and Ulugh Beg that even employed master craftsmen from India. In 1492, at age 14, Babur seized Samarkand for the first time, exclaiming that after a long interval “the plundered and pillaged kingdom once again returned to us.” He measured the city walls and described its famous green gardens in great detail. For Babur, Samarkand was both a magnificent urban paradise and the lost family inheritance his ancestors had built. He identified strongly as a Timurid prince of the Silk Road, rooted in Persian-Turkic culture, and wrote poetry about the meadows and orchards of Samarqand that set the standard for his later Mughal gardens.

    At the same time, Samarkand was a strategic prize. It was said to have been founded by Alexander, and for decades it was contested between Timurids and Uzbeks. Babur grew up amid its splendors: the great madrasa of Registan, the blue-tiled tomb of Timur, and the green gardens by the river Qorovul. These experiences fostered his love of nature and architecture. His memoir shows a cultured voice, writing about plucking roses at dawn and discussing courtly life. Yet he also knew court politics: Ferghana’s rulers constantly sought Samarkand, and young Babur understood from his first days on the throne that power depended on it. Even as he celebrated the city’s “fine buildings” and prosperous markets, he felt the burden of its legacy. In this way, Babur’s identity was forged in Samarkand’s society; he was proud of its learning and beauty, but his very title as Mirza, son of an amir, meant he had a duty to rule it.

    Babur’s Samarkand of 1497–1501 was riven by power struggles and war. The same Uzbek tribal leader Muhammad Shaybani Khan — who had ousted Timurids from Bukhara and Tashkent — now turned his sights on Babur. After Babur’s second capture of Samarkand in 1497, Shaybani united with other Uzbek factions and attacked Timurid princes. The decisive battle came in March 1501 by the river Sar-i-Pul. Babur’s outnumbered cavalry fought “with mail and horse armor” against seasoned Uzbek warriors, but the river crossing turned disastrous: as he retreated, “we had to make the armored horses swim” while arrows rained. Babur barely escaped; he later wrote that within a week he was on the run. In his memoir he notes only in passing that he lost the battle and “could not hold the city.” He abandoned Samarkand after one hundred days, marking a humiliating defeat.

    The aftermath was brutal. Babur’s Timurid troops deserted him, and enemies overran Ferghana and the Oxus provinces. Babur spent months wandering through the mountains of Central Asia, ill-equipped and outnumbered. He wrote of his dire situation: he and a few followers escaped through the passes of the Hindukush, penniless and “on foot, with walking staves,” surviving by foraging. In these exile days his resilience hardened; his diary reflects a will not to despair, writing that “one cannot sit back and watch if events do not go right once or twice.” Without Samarkand, Babur felt “driven from pillar to post,” lamenting in Baburnama how he “lost Samarkand for the sake of Andizhan” and then lost that too. This period of defeat shaped him deeply. He regained composure in exile by focusing on faith and nature — one story even describes him savoring a rare melon to lift his spirits. In short, Babur’s failed bid for Samarkand taught him the fragility of power and the need for new strategies. It also cemented his determination: after regrouping with scattered tribal leaders, he soon set his sights south into India.

    After his exile from Samarkand, Babur turned toward Kabul. In 1504 he captured Kabul almost by chance, finding the Afghan chieftains “ready for a change,” as one account puts it. Kabul became his new base and a gateway to India. For Babur, Afghanistan’s green hills and climate felt a bit like home; he eventually called Kabul his happiest time after childhood. But the lure of the rich plains to the east was inescapable. In 1519 Babur advanced into India, first raiding the Punjab under Ibrahim Lodi. The new country struck him as quite different from Central Asia. His own memoir records a harsh judgment on Hindustan’s climate and society: “Hindustan is a place of little charm. There is no beauty in its people. There is no ice. There are no baths.” He complained of the heat and dust and missed Central Asian comforts like cool water and familiar winter snows. This negative view persisted even after victory, although he pragmatically noted that at least India “is a large country with lots of gold and money.” Babur also admired some features of the land: he wrote of fertile valleys, wild flowers, and splendid mountain views around the cities of Kabul and Lahore. Ultimately, he found India worthy of conquest because of its wealth and the weakness of its local rulers.

    Culturally, Babur’s impressions of India were mixed. He respected the skill of Indian horses and elephants, but he was unimpressed by Indian architecture at first (he was used to Timurid grandeur). Some sources say he remarked that northern India’s traditions were “out of harmony” with his refined Persian-Turkic taste. Yet he also embraced Indian artisans in his camp and adopted new crops (he later wrote with pride that he brought bananas and sugarcane from Kabul into India). In short, Babur’s encounter with India showed him a world that he initially viewed through Central Asian eyes — strange and inferior in his memoir — even as he quickly realized its riches and the opportunities they offered for empire-building.

    One of the most controversial aspects of Babur’s conquest is what his memoirs and actions reveal about his ideology toward religion and local faiths. In India, he did order the plundering of some temples and shrines, often as part of wartime raids. For example, Babur himself records an episode in which he found Jain idols in a hideout near Gwalior and had them mutilated on grounds of “obscenity,” a secular justification. This frankness in Baburnama suggests that Babur saw religious sites primarily as political prizes rather than objects of worship: he “had no reason to hide” destroying a religious image if done for religio-ideological reasons. In other words, when he did attack a temple, he did not disguise it; the lack of mentions of other temple demolitions implies they were not his focus.

    In fact, Babur’s own memoir notably omits any mention of destroying the famous Ayodhya shrine or any grand temple in India. A Hindu legal body argued before the Indian Supreme Court that Baburnama never mentions an Ayodhya demolition or the building of a mosque there, implying Babur likely never visited Ayodhya. Modern historians note that Babur’s motivations were complex: sometimes he demolished a shrine simply because it belonged to a defeated enemy king or contained wealth, not necessarily out of deep Islamic dogma. For example, after defeating Ibrahim Lodi, Babur did plunder the royal treasury of temples and cities but also imposed taxes on Hindus rather than forcing conversions.

    Thus Babur’s “ideology” was not a rigid theology. It was shaped by his Timurid background — in which rulers from Timur onward had seen temple-desecration as a part of conquest — and by his identity as an exiled prince seeking power. He combined devout Sunni faith with practical tolerance: in Kabul he even patronized a local Sufi saint’s hospice. His memoirs dwell far more on gardens, memories of home, and poetry than on jihad. As one observer put it, Babur emerges as a man of contrasts — a “scholar or barbarian,” “nature-loving poet or terror-inspiring warlord,” and the Baburnama makes clear he was both. His formative experiences on the Silk Road — the loss of paradise in Samarkand, the life-and-death struggles with tribes, and the survival in exile — left him worldly and pragmatic. Thus his actions in India (including temple raids) reflected political strategy and personal habit as much as religious zeal.

    Babur’s journey from Central Asia to India was a transformation from Silk Road prince to Mughal emperor. He began as a Timurid mirza, inheriting the legacy of Samarkand’s culture and courtiers, as he joyously recorded in his Baburnama. Defeats by the Uzbeks forced him out of Samarkand and drove him into new lands. Crossing the Hindu Kush and taking Kabul, Babur reinvented himself on the borders of India. His memoir shows his identity evolving: he remained the Turkic-Persian poet of the oases, but he also became a conqueror of the subcontinent. By 1526, when he smashed the Delhi Sultanate at Panipat, Babur was no longer just a Central Asian exile — he was the founder of a new dynasty. His Silk Road upbringing influenced the Mughal world he established: his gardens mimicked Samarqand, his coins bore the names of Timur and Genghis Khan, and his court blended Central Asian and Indian traditions. In conclusion, understanding Babur’s travels along the Silk Road enriches our view of the Mughal Empire. It shows that Babur’s identity was transregional, shaped by connections from Samarkand to Kabul to Delhi. This perspective moves us beyond a narrow “invader narrative” to see Babur as a product of many worlds — a Timurid prince who ultimately became an Indian emperor through a uniquely blended experience of cultures, landscapes, and struggles.

    Inquiry Into Origins Of The Vedic Aryans

    May 28, 2025 · ~11 min read

    Despite genetic, archaeological and linguistic evidence disproving the Aryan Invasion Theory, it still has dominated Indian historiography for over a century. Even today it garners much attention from historians for academic interest and politicians for its socio-political implications. The term “Aryan” generally refers to the Indo-European-speaking peoples of ancient India and Iran. Aryan Invasion Theory (AIT) posited that a warlike Indo-European people invaded India around 1500–1000 BCE. It also denies the indigenous origins of not only the Vedas and Sanskrit but also suggests a strong genetic influence on modern Indians. The theory finds its preliminary foundation not in Indian literature but far, far away in British politics and 19th-century German nationalism. The idea of AIT has largely been abandoned and replaced by the Aryan Migration Theory, which envisages a gradual influx of the Aryans into India rather than a violent conquest. By contrast, the Out-of-India Theory (OIT) holds that Indo-European languages originated in India and spread outward to Iran then to Europe. In this paper we examine archaeological, genetic, and linguistic evidence for these scenarios and place them in context with selected scholars’ views.

    Modern technologies have brought a new dimension into this debate by giving us an ability we never had before. Genetics allows researchers to peek into the very fabric of humans: our DNA. Tony Joseph (Joseph) finds that modern Indians derive their ancestry from at least three sources: indigenous hunter-gatherers, early Iranian agriculturalists, and Steppe pastoralists. The key point for Aryan migration is that the Steppe gene appears in the subcontinent only after around 2000 BCE, or after the Harappan Civilization. As evidence, the first fully analyzed Harappan genome (from a 4,600-year-old skeleton at Rakhigarhi) showed no detectable Steppe gene (Sharma). In Tony Joseph’s words, this confirms “the Arya were not present during the Harappan Civilisation, and came after it” (Amrita Madhukalya). Similarly, Rakhigarhi DNA takes the history back 12,000 years. In Shinde’s words, “hunter-gatherers somewhere in Northwest Asia split into two groups; one came to South Asia and one went to Iran who were genetically similar” (Sharma). The group that went to Iran mixed with Turkish people whereas the group that came to India stayed the same and became the founders of the Harappan Civilization. However, interpretations remain contested. Genetics alone cannot pinpoint migrations or languages. Shinde notes that if a massive Steppe invasion had occurred, we would see a break in Indian genetics (new genes, cultural markers, etc.), but instead South Asian ancestry shows no sudden discontinuity. He argues that later influxes from Central Asia or Persia were absorbed rather than replacing the locals, collapsing the “invasion” notion. Joseph famously used a pizza metaphor to explain that 50–65% of Indian ancestry comes from the “First Indians” (the ancient hunter-gatherer base), with the Harappan-related farmers and later Aryan migrants adding successive layers (Joseph). Therefore, the presence of Steppe ancestry need not validate colonial invasion narratives; it simply reflects complex prehistory for many regions. DNA is in no way the ultimate key to unlocking the mystery as there are multiple folds and dimensions to this complex issue. It does however completely refute the notion of Aryans arriving as “superior conquerors” from Europe, undercut by evidence that Indians had a flourishing civilization before Steppe genes arrived. Moreover, genetics is a new field which needs time to mature. To date there has been only one site and just one skeleton which has been studied. One person’s DNA at just one site can’t be representative of all the almost 2 billion inhabitants of the Indian subcontinent. Therefore a fuller picture will involve multiple genetic studies supported by archaeological evidence.

    The origin of Indians has its roots in the Harappan Civilization as established by contemporary genetics. Thus, it is imperative for us to examine the archaeological sites and artifacts found there to use them as pieces for this puzzle. Archaeological excavations at ancient sites in modern-day Pakistan and northern India suggest long-term indigenous development of the Harappan Civilization. Excavations at major Harappan sites such as Rakhigarhi, Harappa, and Mohenjo-daro reveal continuous urban planning, pottery styles, and ritual infrastructure (baths, granaries, fire altars) from as early as 6000 BCE through 1900 BCE, with no abrupt cultural rupture that would mark a foreign conquest (Sangam Talks). Horses play a significant role in this entire narrative; it was put forth by the colonial historians that the Aryans came to India atop their horse chariots and waged a war on the native inhabitants. The horse here is key, as it was suggested that the horse was introduced into India by the invading Aryans. The absence of horses in the Harappan sites was cited as major proof of this theory. However this has been refuted by excavations at Surkotada (a Harappan site), where Hungarian archaeologist Sandor Bokonyi said there were bones of horse present. Moreover at Lothal and Mohenjo-daro (Harappan sites) toy horse figurines were excavated. The sparse nature of horse remains at Harappan sites also goes against the theory of a horse-riding Aryan invasion. This is because it indicates a lack of sudden influx of charioted warriors. It is said that the Aryans brought the already composed Vedas into India. In the Rigveda there are multiple references to the river Saraswati; interestingly satellite and geological studies show the Ghaggar–Hakra (Sarasvati) River system dried up by around 1900 BCE, yet Vedic hymns describe it as a great and sacred river — suggesting that the Vedic culture may post-date the Mature Harappan, rather than arriving to displace it (Danino). It also provides a compelling argument against the theory that the Vedas were composed outside India and then brought in later through conquest. Lokmanya Tilak saw evidence of Vedic fire altars in Late Harappan settlements along the Sarasvati basin, indicating indigenous continuity of ritual practice (Tilak). The critics of AIT provide evidence for OIT by citing the discovery of Harappan sites at Shahr-i-Sokhta in Iran and Gonur in Turkmenistan. These claims lack scientific backing and require further investigation. Overall archaeology does provide adequate empirical proofs and evidence which are tangible in nature. However some subtleties and nuances are not captured through artifacts; some perish to the test of time and some are lost forever. Therefore an investigation is only complete when linguistics is brought into the picture, as language is an area around which this debate heavily revolves.

    As the British entered India and learned the languages here they started noticing similarities between languages of India, Persia and Europe. There are striking similarities in the vocabulary and grammar which are too frequent and too striking to be considered coincidental (Sangam Talks). Thus was born the concept of “Indo-European Languages.” This theory was extrapolated further to suggest that all these languages and the people who use them descended from a single population source and a progenitor language. The Aryan Migration narrative has been grounded in comparative philology: Sanskrit, Greek, Latin and other Indo-European languages show deep similarities traced to a common Proto-Indo-European (PIE) ancestor. The PIE was located in the Steppe region of Eastern Europe. Critics, most notably Shrikant Talageri, argue that this is not true (Talageri). The oldest Sanskrit texts, the Vedas, are tied to the Indian subcontinent’s flora, fauna and geography. All the poetic description of landscape and weather suggests an Indian setting rather than a European or Central Asian one. One of the most prominent gods in the Rigveda is Indra, the god of thunder in the Hindu pantheon. The development of a deity based on the natural phenomenon of thunder is only warranted when the poets are exposed to thunder. This is only possible in India and not in the Steppe region. India annually has a monsoon season which brings about thunderstorms, however the Steppe region is completely dry and void of heavy rainfall. The Vedas themselves do give a detailed account of the political situation at that time; there is recounted a battle of 10 kings and all the infighting between the early Vedic tribes. However, there is no mention of the Vedic tribes invading a foreign country and fighting with non-Vedic people. If the invasion were true, there certainly should have been such references. In conclusion linguistics does serve to give researchers a literary resource that they can use in their study. Like genetics and archaeology, linguistics as well is fickle in its ability to provide concrete proof to support either version of the theory.

    Overall, the entire debate around Aryan Invasion Theory is greatly politically spirited. Every academic personality and researcher that is referred to certainly carries with them a political leaning. In contemporary India, the AIT supporters can be classified as left-leaning on the political spectrum and the AIT refuters / OIT acceptors are usually from the right wing. In a landscape so susceptible to political headwinds, a true researcher and a person with the will to truth has to navigate through agenda and propaganda to unravel the real hidden truth. The words of J. Sai Deepak stick with me; he clearly identifies that the realm of academia in colonial India was motivated by missionary vigour, therefore the introduction of AIT was used as a tool to push the repressive agenda against the indigenous culture of India. Understanding the real motives is crucial to get a complete picture.

    Epistolaries are cool, so was our oldest modern feminist Pamela!

    May 10, 2025 · ~8 min read

    Pamela is an epistolary novel written in 1740 by Samuel Richardson. This novel was conceived during a particular period in English society when the established social, cultural, and political norms were beginning to lose their foundation and were ready for profound change. This transformation is explored in the book through the conflict between a 15-year-old servant girl, Pamela, and her master, Mr. B, who tries in every possible way to become sexually intimate with her. However, the story is much deeper than a tale of sexual tension between two characters. The novel reflects the conflict and struggle between different social classes, particularly the rising lower classes and the established aristocracy. It depicts the evolution of moral values and consciousness.

    Eighteenth-century English society was highly sectarian and divided into multiple socio-economic groups. While monetary worth was a key factor in distinguishing between classes, an even more significant element was the concept of “gentility,” an inherent characteristic of a person believed to be shaped by their social standing. Gentility, combined with wealth, allowed people to be identified as members of one class or another. The novel portrays the unstable relationship between the gentility of aristocrats and their social status, prompting the reader to question whether the gentry truly possess the moral worth required to justify their elevated position.

    Mr. B is consistently referred to as “Squire,” a term used for members of the gentry in old England. In one of Pamela’s early letters, she writes, “To be sure I did nothing but curchee and cry, and was all in Confusion, at his Goodness. Indeed he is the best of Gentlemen, I think!” Her use of “Squire” and “gentleman” reflects both his higher social status and Pamela’s respect for him. However, despite this status, he fails to justify his position due to his sexual advances and demeaning behavior toward Pamela. This is captured in the disturbing line: “I found myself in his Arms, quite void of Strength, and he kissed me two or three times, as if he would have eaten me.” Pamela later questions his moral worth, asking, “Is this the noble gentleman, who was so admired by everybody? O how are all appearances deceitful!”

    In contrast to Mr. B, Pamela’s gentility is not inherited but cultivated through her manners and religious upbringing by her parents. As Mrs. Jervis says to Pamela, “You are a good girl, and have more prudence and discretion than many ladies of quality.” Pamela herself is aware of her moral worth: “I may be a poor girl, but I am honest, and would not do a bad thing for the world.” This ideal is taken to the extreme by her father, who insists, “My dear child, let me beg of you to resolve to lose your life rather than your virtue.” All of this “virtue,” rooted in her religiosity, allows her to fit into the upper-class household, even though she does not come from that social strata.

    The conflict over “gentility” is further emphasized when one considers that Mr. B is associated with a class that supposedly elevates him above others. His refined speech and courteous behavior appear gentle, but his immoral actions undermine his character. Pamela observes this contradiction: “He speaks so soft, and is so courteous sometimes, that one would think he could not be so vile as he is.”

    Understanding Mr. B’s lack of morality is incomplete without acknowledging the moral and social standards by which he is judged. In the mid-18th century, for the first time in literature, a member of the aristocracy was evaluated from the perspective of a lower-class character, using a deeply religious Christian framework. Pamela uses the Bible to measure Mr. B’s moral worth. This relegation of the aristocracy to the same moral laws as the common people was new and radical. Until then, the upper classes were accustomed to acting without consequence. Mr. B has had previous illicit relationships with maids and servants—Pamela is not the first he has pursued. This is revealed through the account of Sally Godfrey. His behavior toward lower-class women is not an isolated case; it reflects a common pattern among the gentry.

    When Pamela hopes to gain help from the local gentry to escape Mrs. Jewkes, they decline, stating, “there was no honourable Way but Marriage.” This reveals how widely accepted the authority of the master over the servant was, and how the only “honourable” path for Pamela was to accept Mr. B. The morality of both the aristocracy and lower classes is shown to be deeply skewed. Pamela represents a shift from conventional social norms to a stricter moral code. This is why Mr. B is bewildered when Pamela refuses to submit to him, even after he offers her luxury. He exclaims, “I will make a Gentlewoman of you, if you are obliging, and don’t stand in your own Light; and so saying, he put his Arm about me, and kiss’d me!” Due to his aristocratic upbringing, Mr. B expects Pamela to be compliant, seeing obedience as part of her role. However, his entitled worldview is challenged by Pamela’s strong sense of moral agency.

    When Mr. B says, “I will make a gentlewoman out of you,” he implies that either marrying him or becoming his mistress would elevate Pamela’s social status. From the aristocratic perspective, social worth is tied to wealth and lineage. Pamela, on the other hand, sees social worth as stemming from religiosity and moral strength. It is her Christian values that grant her gentility and make her worthy of higher status—not her birth.

    The ascendance of the bourgeoisie over the aristocracy on the topic of cultural mores is represented through the character of Pamela. She looks down upon her master and is herself confused how a supposedly higher person from an upper class with a greater sense of gentility could behave in such a lowly manner. “I hope I never shall find him to act unworthy of his Character; for what could he get by ruining such a poor young Creature as me”—this strongly represents how she feels about Mr. B. She expects him to be virtuous and true to his gentleman status. She uses their difference in social standing as a shield to guard herself from the Squire’s sexual advances. “Well may I forget that I am your servant, when you forget what belongs to a master,” Pamela has a strong sense of the boundaries she has and she knows these boundaries are not to be crossed by anyone. Moreover, as they both are from two different classes the Squire cannot engage in this manner with Pamela. This is seen through the sentence, “But I say nothing yet of your caution, or of my own uneasiness, to Mrs. Jervis … from the great distance between such a gentleman, and so poor a girl.” The stress on the “distance” is important here and is reflected in the entire novel which portrays the struggle between the classes and the blurry ranking of them. This book brings about a change in society, where it will urge the readers to reimagine who stands where on the social scale.

    Notes on Pride and Prejudice

    Apr 13, 2025 · ~5 min read

    Rough reading notes — jotted as I went.

    The book feels rough going; the book is funny. Elizabeth is satirical and critical about the faults of the people around her, whereas her sisters aren’t. The fundamental way in which the author values people is sensibility. The sillier they are, the more they care about the social structure.

    The way in which Elizabeth reacts to the comment is playful with it. She has a very robust sense of herself. The people in this book live in a society that has not been psychologized yet — in contrast to the other women who are very concerned with marriage and attracting men.

    Page 7: Bingley is talking to Darcy. “Not handsome enough to tempt me.” Elizabeth is engaged in observing other people and judging them. This leads to the structure of the narrative style of this novel. Literary critics distinguish between the author and the narratorial voice. This voice takes onto itself the task and the pretension of the judgment of her culture. In Pamela the author is criticising the way that an aristocratic man has rights over a woman. The narratorial voice manifests a narratorial eye.

    Elizabeth judges in the same way as Jane Austen does. She doesn’t see everything that the narrator sees, but still her moral judgements are considerably correct. Elizabeth, when she is over at Bingley’s, is most concerned about Jane; she is very happy that Bingley is interested in Jane. She is not thinking about herself — she wants her sister to do well.

    To make an alliance between Darcy and Elizabeth would be to weaken the lineage of Darcy. He does not want to be attracted to her, but he is attracted to her. He is a serious person and he takes this matter of being attracted to a person seriously.

    The first thing that is asked in romantic comedies is how much money a man has. This is an obsessive concern of the bourgeoisie. What is a bourgeois woman supposed to be like? Is a question. What kinds of qualities is she supposed to have — like playing piano, speaking a foreign language? This is the discussion that is going on. What is the proper distinction between man and woman?

    Social extremes: Mrs. Bennet at one end and Darcy at the other. Darcy is vulgar and unclassy. Emma wants to distinguish between pure class and social class.

    The author is supremely intelligent; she understands what qualities a human should have, if based on intelligence. She places the highest value on the “sense” and “intelligence” of the character. Elizabeth has the highest emotional intelligence and Mrs. Bennet has the lowest. This book feels like a blurry camera which is slowly coming into focus. Richardson is on a much smaller level of intelligence when it comes to social and human interactions.

    Mrs. Lucas is a good example of how you act as a woman: she marries Mr. Collins for money, security, and her own house. Even though Collins is an insupportable twit, nonetheless Mrs. Lucas’s goal was to get married as she was 27 and getting older. Elizabeth’s lack of regard for grabbing such a wonderful opportunity of marriage. Elizabeth outlines what a modern girl is going to look like.

    Mrs. Phillips perceives Collins to be impressive — pompous, and the way he gives long speeches. Satire is being made about certain types of people. Mrs. Bennet cannot value her most intelligent daughter. Mr. Bingley does not condescend to Mr. Bennet.

    “I like intrinsic over instrumental value of morality”, Kant probably

    May 31, 2026 · ~7 min read

    Kant argues that the rules of morality are categorical and absolute which are applicable to all always. In this paper, I will reconstruct his argument on acting from the motive of duty and not on consequences. Next, I will consider how duty is related to the categorical imperative and then explain Kant’s formula of universal law to formulate the categorical imperative. Further, I reconstruct the case of a person making a commitment without any intention to fulfil it which is called the lying promise. I will then expand on Kant’s explanation of the immorality of the lying promise. Finally, I will evaluate how Kant through the formulation of universal law is successful in explaining the immorality of lying. My main reason is the objective framework of Kant’s argument and his push towards acting out of duty rather than consequences. I argue that rightness of an action precedes its outcomes, and that morality derives intrinsically and not instrumentally. Therefore it is acting out of duty and having a good will which leads to an action being moral.

    For Kant the rationalizing and the decision making part in us is the free will, and out of all other goods it is only the good which is “good without qualifications”. As Kant put it, “It is impossible to think of anything at all in the world, or indeed even beyond it, that could be considered good without limitation except a good will” (4:393). When the will acts from the motive of duty and out of respect for a universal law it is only then that the will becomes good. This theory of duty makes Kant’s morality deontological as the actions are freely chosen from duty and not only in accordance with duty. This distinction is crucial to understand moral worth. A shopkeeper who does not overcharge an inexperienced customer on the grounds of protecting his reputation though does the right thing does not do it for the right reason, therefore his action is not grounded in duty and thus does not carry moral worth (4:397). Actions which carry moral worth are grounded in respect for the universal moral law and not on the consequences. This moral law is what Kant calls the categorical imperative. This holds true in all cases and for everyone, and this is an obligation which is demanded by pure reason. The categorical imperative is distinct from the hypothetical imperative which commands an action as a means to an end. If you want to get good at playing the drums, then the action of practicing drums would be the means to achieve that ends. This is not a categorical imperative as this maxim of practising loses its objective once the end of getting good at drums is abandoned. Thus, Kant argues, morality must not rest on imperatives of the conditional kinds but must rest on universally binding obligations. A categorical imperative is therefore necessary in itself and completely independent of the consequences. This is formulated through the formula of universal law, “I ought never to act except in such a way that I could also will that my maxim should become a universal law” (4:402). This formulation allows me to test whether my action is done in accordance with duty and assess the morality of it. Before acting, I isolate the maxim of my action and ask whether I want to will that everyone act on it, myself included.

    Kant has showcased the use of the categorical imperative to determine the morality of an action using the case of the lying promise. Someone finds themselves in need of borrowing money, they know they won’t be able to repay the money but they will only be lent money if they promise to repay it in a determinate time. In this case is making the false promise irrational is the question? Applying the formula of universal law the maxim of this action would be “when I believe myself to be in need of money I shall borrow money and promise to repay it, even though I know that this will never happen” (4:422). In a world where this maxim was made universal everyone would make false promises and the world would cease to function properly. Promises will not mean anything, leading to distrust and the collapse of institutions which rely on them for proper functioning. “I could indeed will the lie, but by no means will a universal law to lie; for in accordance with such a law there would properly be no promises at all, therefore no rational being would will this world into existence” (4:403). Kant says this maxim cannot be made universal and marks the lying promise as a violation of duty and fundamentally immoral. The immorality of the lying promise according to Kant is completely independent of whether lying produces negative consequences in the long run, or whether it disrupts the psychic harmony of the mind or if it does not follow a social contract. Immorality is the product of willing something which is not categorical.

    Kant’s grounding of morality in something objective and categorical is the strongest feature of his moral theory. The right precedes the good and ethics cannot be dependent exclusively on the outcomes. What acting from duty delivers is a criterion for the right, and this need does not coincide with the good. If I lie to turn away a murderer from his victim the consequence is good however the act is neither right nor categorically imperative. Every consequentialist has trailing conditions; lie to save a life, lie when no other harm follows, lie when you protect someone. Each qualification invites the next and this cascade of ifs and buts leads to the decadence of the moral theory. The maxim “I will not lie out of duty to a universal law” is more logically consistent than “I will not lie because it may produce a negative outcome.” This consistency is what sets Kant apart from everyone else. “Lie when a life is at stake” cannot distinguish the innocent’s life from the guilty, this leniency allows the murderer’s accomplice to keep his partner from punishment by lying which is plainly not a good outcome. A categorical block on lying prevents this and binds everyone and every case together. However, the categorical imperative as put by Kant does have a real difficulty. What truly makes a maxim categorical, and more importantly the framing of a maxim out of an action. Universalizability is dependent on the description of the maxim, if the maxim is not true to the action it creates a big logical inconsistency and Kant’s theory fails. Moreover, the narrow maxims are more universalizable and broader maxims are too easy to universalize. This is answered partly through the other formulation of the categorical imperative. Namely the formula of humanity, the irrationality of an action is determined by whether you disrespect the humanity of a rational agent. If you use someone as a means to an end then it is immoral. This too has problems with regards to what exactly qualifies as a “rational being” or a “human”, who is worthy of humanity and who is not. This fundamental discriminatory bias was observed in Kant’s own writings.

    The case of the lying promise shows the success of Kant’s moral theory while also highlighting some of his drawbacks. His focus on the categorical nature of morality and the independence from consequences were shown in this paper to be the cornerstone of his theory. I showed how Kant through the formula of universal law of the categorical imperative is able to show the irrationality of the lying promise. I illustrated that the right precedes the good and that an action is right regardless of the consequences it produces when it is done out of the duty to a categorical law. Overall, Kant has given us a solid and consistent foundation for moral reasoning grounded in objectivity.

    Plato never truly justified the justness of justice.

    Apr 25, 2026 · ~8 min read

    Plato argues that a life well lived is one of justice and morality which allows one to achieve eudaimonia. In this paper, I will reconstruct his argument of the three parts of the soul, and what their roles and functions are. Next, I consider the meaning of justice and how the harmonic functioning of the three parts of the soul gives rise to justice. Finally, I will evaluate how the argument of psychic harmony does convince me to live a life of justice and morality. My main reason is the revisionist account of justice put forth by Plato, which allows me to view justice in an internal lens and as a true ideal. Moreover, the idea of psychic harmony provides me with an internal standard to evaluate the true value of happiness, which further solidifies my belief in living a moral life. However, I argue that Plato’s reliance on comparative arguments ultimately proves justice is valuable for what it produces rather than for what it is.

    In the Republic, Plato gives a revisionist account of justice, one that challenges the conventional view still prevalent today. “Most people would put it [justice] in the unpleasant class, which we should cultivate in return for payment and reputation, on account of public opinion, but which purely for itself is to be avoided like the plague” (Plato’s Republic 358a). This is what Glaucon said about how most people perceive justice to be. Glaucon divides types of goods into three kinds. The first are the goods that carry value for their own sake and not out of desire for its results such as appreciating beauty and aesthetics, enjoying music, tasting something exquisite. Second, are the goods that we value both for themselves and for its consequences such as “thinking, seeing, being healthy” (Plato’s Republic 357c pp. 37). Third are goods we would not choose for their own sake but only for the benefits which result from them. Physical exercise, undergoing medical treatment, and working a job would fall under this category. This would be the unpleasant class and according to Glaucon and the conventional view, justice would be a third kind of good. It contains value because people can cultivate a reputation or get a desired outcome if they act just. For example, a person will always pay their credit card bills on time to maintain their credit score, the just act of clearing your debt is performed with an outcome of positive social credibility and not merely just to do that action for its own sake. Plato disagrees with Glaucon and places justice as a second kind of good, one that is good both for its own sake and for its consequences. To prove this, he gives a revised account of what justice actually is.

    Plato does this through his theory of the tripartite nature of a person’s soul. These are the three parts of the mind that guide a person’s moral decision making. This theory is based on the principle of opposites. Plato explains that one and the same thing cannot be in two contrary states at once (Plato’s Republic 436c). If one were to move their arm but not their leg we don’t say they are moving and still at the same time. We instead say that the part of the body that is the arm is moving and the leg is still. This can be applied to the mind as well, where if one experiences different things at the same time then it must be the work of multiple different parts. For example, when I eat ice cream, one part of my brain enjoys the pleasure associated with eating something sweet, however a part of my mind is anxious and distressed by the knowledge of the negative effects of sugar on my body. This is where Plato introduces the three parts of the soul. First is reason which specializes in governing the mind. It has the knowledge of what is good and bad. Second is spirit which is the strength of will to stand by conviction. Third is appetite, which is the source of all desire for bodily and other pleasures. Every part has their own respective function, however a proper functioning of the mind requires cooperation between the three parts. Appetite is necessary for bodily sustenance and survival. It is the reason which structures and constrains these appetites and spirit provides the will to follow through with the constraints put on appetite by reason. This framework allows Plato to explain what justice is.

    The psychic harmony which results from the complete cooperation of the three parts of the soul constitutes justice (Plato’s Republic 441e). It is an internal state arising from reason governing the soul, appetite being directed toward bodily needs, and spirit enforcing reason’s directions (Plato’s Republic 443d). Injustice, on the other hand, is the complete opposite of internal harmony as Plato puts it, “some sort of civil war between these three elements, a rebellion by one part of the soul against the whole.” (Plato’s Republic 444b). Maintenance of psychic harmony is essential for living a good life, as without it a person is perpetually at odds with their own decisions. Psychic harmony is only ensured when one’s actions are dictated by a mind ruled by reason. When that natural constitution is ruined, life does not seem worth living, even with all the pleasures one may indulge in (Plato’s Republic 445a).

    Psychic harmony results from a lack of internal conflict which Plato claims to open up the path to a good life. This account of justice is used by him to answer the central question of the Republic: ‘Why be moral?’. The arbitrage between the quality of life of an unjust and a just person is highlighted by Plato to answer this question of morality. Plato characterizes people into five different types based on the internal workings of their mind. Timocrats are ruled by spirit and chase victory and honor, oligarchs are ruled by all the necessary appetites like wealth and material possessions, democrats are ruled by unnecessary appetites but their desire is regulable. Then there’s the tyrant who is exclusively ruled by a relentless and excess appetite to consume and indulge in anything and everything that generates pleasure. They will lie, cheat, steal, engage in vices and lust for pleasure. “They have no choice but to lay their hands on anything they can lay their hands on” (Plato’s Republic 574a). On the complete opposite end are the kings or the republicans, it’s only them with a soul in perfect harmony as they are purely led by reason.

    The tyrant, though able to fulfil all their desires and indulge in every pleasure, will still lead a terrible life. Tyrants are in bondage to their desire and are stuck in an eternal pursuit of pleasure. They structure their entire lives in order to ensure the state of constant pleasure. This need for stability and certainty in their lives makes them slaves to objects and people that ensure they can get what they want. It’s a tragic irony, tyrants never truly lead a life of freedom. They are always trying to exert force, coerce or flatter others to gain what they want (Plato’s Republic 576a). History provides us with examples of dictators who seemingly hold absolute power live in constant paranoia, purging allies and surrounding themselves with loyalists, never able to trust anyone precisely because their power rests on coercion rather than genuine loyalty. This insatiable appetite may generate power, but it will never quench the thirst of desire.

    Plato posits an objective ordering of pleasures derived from the internal nature of humans. He introduces the concept of a competent judge, those who have experienced all three types of pleasure and can therefore make a valid comparative judgment. The competent judges order pleasures by their varying degree of permanence (Plato’s Republic 580d). Pleasures from appetite are fleeting and never truly satisfied, one may eat a hearty meal with all the food their heart may desire but give them a few hours and they would be hungry and looking for food again. Spirited pleasures are derived from victory, honor and reputation — though more permanent they still depend on external factors. Someone is victorious only after someone else’s loss, one gains reputation and respect only in someone else’s eyes. This need for another is what diminishes the value and the permanence of spirited pleasures. Rational pleasures are derived from the knowledge of truth, this is the very pleasure caused by psychic harmony. This pleasure is permanent and this state of eudaimonia is valued the highest by Plato. Only the king who has achieved eudaimonia will be permanently happy, whereas the tyrant will be a slave to seeking out a fleeting pleasure. This makes the life of the tyrant miserable. To be truly happy and lead a good life, requires eudaimonia and which requires one to be moral.

    Plato’s argument in favor of living a just life is heavily comparative in nature. By contrasting the tyrant’s enslavement to desire with the king’s internal harmony, he provides a compelling reason for a skeptic to prefer the life of justice over injustice. However, this comparative analysis does not establish what Plato originally intended to prove, justice being the second kind of good, valuable for both its own sake and for its consequences. The comparative argument presented by him only succeeds in convincing me to choose a just life however is subpar in highlighting the inherent good value of justice. The tyrant is miserable due to paranoia and insatiable craving whereas the just person achieves eudaimonia, this is a convincing argument in favor of justice. However this actually demonstrates that justice is worth pursuing only because of what it produces. The value is contained in eudaimonia and justice is only the means to achieve it. For me this puts justice closer to the second type of good, which is not what Plato intends. This is like exercise producing good health, health is the true value carrier and exercise is just the means to achieve it. Justice is a condition which is only valued for the flourishing in humans it generates. This conflict for me can be resolved if justice were not to cause eudaimonia but instead constitutes eudaimonia. However, Plato’s heavy reliance on comparative claims such as tyrant vs king, freedom vs enslavement, ranking of pleasures again pushes the readers to choose justice for the consequences and not due to inherent value in justice. To truly establish justice as good for its own sake, Plato would need to show that justice is worth even when no eudaimonia and permanent pleasure is produced. I firmly believe in Plato’s argument for living a good and a moral life. However, I refuse to classify justice and a moral life to be inherently good.

    Finally, in this paper I reconstructed Plato’s theory of the tripartite soul, his account of justice and his comparison of the tyrannical and just life. I argued that Plato convincingly demonstrated the just life being better than the unjust life on the consequential value of justice. However he does not fully prove its intrinsic value. Further exploration on the nature of justice and eudaimonia and their relationship will unravel more about Plato’s arguments.

    I read Meghadūta, but did I read Meghadūta?

    Jun 18, 2026 · ~6 min read

    As is the case with YouTube recommendations — and with life — you never know what might stumble in front of you. So a video from Vikas Divyakirti talking about Kalidasa vs. Tulsidas popped up in my feed. I was pleasantly surprised by the decisiveness with which Vikas called Kalidasa the greatest literary figure India has produced. My initial reaction was that surely he meant the greatest in a single language, or in a particular period; but he meant it in the truest sense. Kalidasa, for him — and, as I soon learned, by consensus among literary critics — is the greatest literary figure Bharat has produced. I refused to believe it: there was no way he beats Vyasa, or the countless Vedic sages, or Tukaram. I decided to trust Vikas's authority and accept the claim — but I wanted to test it for myself.

    Coincidentally, around the same time, a friend invited me to a function at my university for the launch of a Sanskrit edition of the Gita Govinda. That, coupled with my initial intrigue about poetic Sanskrit literature, pushed me to pick up the   copy of Meghadūta.

    I genuinely expected the book to be far bigger than it turned out to be. I also already knew the main premise: a man asking a cloud to be his messenger. Even so, I was struck by how the whole story — or rather the poem — is structured. I was impressed that all of it is a single monologue delivered by the yaksha. I liked how it splits into distinct parts: the first twelve verses set up the premise; the next seventy or eighty give an exquisite account of Bharat's landscape; then a section paints his hometown of Alaka. It all culminates in his message to his nāyikā, which speaks exquisitely of love and separation and offers a wonderful portrait of a beautiful woman.

    I am a visual reader. What truly impresses me in a piece of writing is how well the author can generate a world and express it. I love metaphors and similes, and Kalidasa has nailed them in Meghadūta. His way of crafting a story and building images out of all of nature — and everything in it — is genuinely wonderful. He uses the landscape of Bharat, its cities, its women, its flora and fauna to build an impeccable world, and he stays true to the erotic, sensual nature that underscores it all. The double-meaning quality of everything is what sells the poem to me. I keep thinking back to the original listeners and readers of this work, wondering what their outlook on this kind of writing must have been. I am amazed by the aesthetics involved: peacocks, flowers, trees, and wonderful olfactory, auditory, and tactile sensations are evoked to produce a living piece of literature. The overarching use of a cloud as a messenger is itself a testament to Bharat's wondrous monsoon, and how it stands for the onset of the season of love. The focus on the cloud drinking water from rivers is a metaphor for its intercourse with the river-goddess personification — beautiful, again, because this poet does not shy away from indulging the kāma aspect of life.

    It is the non-linear, structurally and metaphorically convoluted sentences that appeal to me. And yet everything I read — and ultimately appreciated — was through an English translation. Though I read the translator's foreword, and he did say he would try to retain the structure, I still felt a major disconnect between me and Kalidasa. I didn't read Kalidasa, nor did I read a Sanskrit poem. I read something else — a lesser copy, through a lesser language. I believe Sanskrit provides a far more useful framework for the literary style I really enjoy: in it, the double meaning is not an accident or something that merely happens, as in English — it is intentional. That is what I think I missed. The fact that every Sanskrit kāvya is composed in a meter only adds to the inauthenticity of reading an English translation in prose. So I will revisit Meghadūta. I will read it in Sanskrit — and only then will I truly be able to appreciate, or even understand, why people say Kalidasa is the greatest literary figure Bharat has given birth to.
